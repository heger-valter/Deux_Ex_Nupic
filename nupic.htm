NuPIC: Numenta Platform for Intelligent Computing.

 

Hierarchal Temporal Memory (HTM) Cortical Learning Algorithm (CLA) was developed by Jeff Hawkins and others, funded by the money he made from his previous start-ups, PalmPilot and HandSpring.  Earlier in his life, Hawkins wanted to study neurobiology and to build computers inspired by the architecture of the mammalian neocortex.  Unfortunately, he was unable to find research support in that area, so worked as an engineer and entrepreneur in the field of hand held devices.

Basically, the human neocortex is about the size of a dinner table cloth and is rumpled up the top layer of the brain.

At a microscopic level, there are about 4 primary layers of cells.  This pattern replicates itself and seems to be uniform regardless of the sensors or localization. 

This model of the neocortex is represented as a data structure of linked nodes consisting of 4 layers.  The bottom layers represent the sensor input feeds and propagate information up the next higher layer.  The top three higher levels propagate information up and down to interconnect nodes.  However, each cluster is only connected to 3% of the clusters in the above and below layers.  This architecture is called the Sparse Distributed Representation.  The highest level makes classification decisions.  Hawkins refers to this data structure as Hierarchal Temporal Memory. 

  Hawkins 2004


It is interesting in that this data structure can be used to detect patterns in time and space.

 

This seems to be common approach which I have read in Marvin Minsky's book "Society of the Mind" in 1987 and in the neural networks algorithms which arose during that time.

 

Recently, Hawkins makes the claim that within 5 years, this model will give rise to true computer intelligence. 

In order to effectively scale the algorithm, Hawkins has secured DARPA funding to build specialized processors which implements this model in hardware.

 

However, partly due to completive pressure from Google's proprietary "Deep Mind", Hawkins decided share the algorithm with the open source community under the GPL, so that others may contribute to the development, primarily so that he can find sufficient open source applications and testers who can try and extend this approach to other applications.  Twice a year, Numenta holds a weekend long hackathon in Redwood, CA (road trip anyone).  The more interesting applications are recorded for viewing on http://www.youtube.com. Numenta has an online weekly office hour discussion. 

 

Also, there are lots of issues in the development of this idea which can be done cost effectively by a large group of users.  He compares his approach to that used to open source computer technology in the 1980's.


see  http://lists.numenta.org/pipermail/nupic_lists.numenta.org/2013-June/000327.html
